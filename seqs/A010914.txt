%F A010914 It is not hard to show that A010914 indeed satisfies the following simple linear recurrence relation: a(n) = 4*a(n-1) - 2*a(n-2).
%F A010914 Let a(n) = A010914(n) be the sequence defined by the recurrence a(n) = floor( a(n-1)^2/a(n-2) + 1/2 ) and initial terms a(0)=5 and a(1)=17. Define a sequence b(n) by the recurrence b(n) = 4*b(n-1) - 2*b(n-2) and the same initial terms b(0)=a(0)=5 and b(1)=a(1)=17. We want to prove that a(n)=b(n) for all n.
%F A010914 The main trick is that instead of proving that the sequence a() satisfies the recurrence for the sequence b(), we will do it the other way around: prove that b() satisfied the recurrence for a(). This will imply that these two sequences coincide.
%F A010914 To prove that b() satisfies the recurrence for a(), it is enough to show that abs( b(n-1)^2 - b(n)*b(n-2) ) < b(n-2)/2. To do this, we will use the explicit formula for b(n):
%F A010914 b(n) = (10+7*sqrt(2))/4 * (2+sqrt(2))^n +  (10-7*sqrt(2))/4 * (2-sqrt(2))^n.
%F A010914 Explicitly computing abs( b(n-1)^2 - b(n)*b(n-2) ), one finds that it equals 2^n times a constant. At the same time, b(n) grows as (2+sqrt(2))^n (up to a constant) which allows one to easily prove (e.g., by induction on n) that abs( b(n-1)^2 - b(n)*b(n-2) ) < b(n-2)/2. (End)
