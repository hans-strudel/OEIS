%N A003010 A Lucas-Lehmer sequence: a(0) = 4; for n>0, a(n) = a(n-1)^2 - 2.
%C A003010 Albert Beiler states (page 228 of Recreations in the Theory of Numbers): D. H. Lehmer modified Lucas's test to the relatively simple form: If and only if 2^n-1 divides a(n-2) then 2^n-1 is a prime, otherwise it is composite. Since 2^3 - 1 is a factor of a(1) = 14, 2^3 - 1 = 7 is a prime. - _Gary W. Adamson_, Jun 07 2003

%F A003010 From Ulrich Sondermann, Sep 04 2006: (Start)
%F A003010 To generate the n-th number in the sequence: let x = 2^(n-1), a = 2, b = sqrt(3). Take every other term of the binomial expansion (a+b)^x times 2.
%F A003010 E.g., for the 4th term: x = 2^(4-1) = 8, the binomial expansion is: a^8 + 7a^7 b + 28a^6 b^2 + 56a^5 b^3 + 70a^4 b^4 + 56a^3 b^5 + 28a^2 b^6 + 7a b^7 + b^8, every other term times 2: 2(a^8 + 28a^6 b^2 + 70a^4 b^4 + 28a^2 b^6 + b^8) = 2(256 + (28)(64)(3) + (70)(16)(9) + (28)(4)(27) + 81) = 2(18817) = 37634. (End)
