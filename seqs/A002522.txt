%C A002522 Also, except for the first term, numbers that cannot be expressed as a perfect power, i.e., x^2 + 1 != y^n for all x,y,n > 1. Proof: We assume the truth of the following theorem. Proofs can be found in elementary texts on number theory and online. Theorem I: A number N is a sum of two squares if and only if all prime factors of N of the form 4m+3 have even exponents.
%C A002522 We are now ready to prove x^2 + 1 != y^n for all x,y,n > 1. We assume equality and seek a contradiction for n even and n odd. If n is even = 2k, x^2 + 1 = y^2k = (y^k)^2 and (y^k - x)(y^k + x) = 1. This implies y^k-x = y^k+x = 1 or 2x = 0 contrary to x > 1. So n must be odd for equality to hold.
%C A002522 Then x^2+1 = y^(2k+1) implies all prime factors of y, including those of the form 4m+3 are raised to an odd exponent contrary to Theorem I. So we have shown x^2+1 = y^n is false for n even or n odd. Therefore x^2 + 1 != y^n as was desired. (End)
