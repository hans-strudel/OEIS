%N A002618 a(n) = n*phi(n).
%C A002618 Also Euler phi function of n^2.
%C A002618 For n >= 3, a(n) is also the size of the automorphism group of the dihedral group of order 2n. This automorphism group is isomorphic to the group of transformations x -> ax + b, where a, b and x are integers modulo n and a is coprime to n. Its order is n*phi(n). - Ola Veshta (olaveshta(AT)my-deja.com), Mar 18 2001
%C A002618 Order of metacyclic group of polynomial of degree n. - _Artur Jasinski_, Jan 22 2008
%C A002618 It appears that this sequence gives the number of permutations of 1, 2, 3, ..., n that are arithmetic progressions modulo n. - _John W. Layman_, Aug 27 2008
%C A002618 The conjecture by Layman is correct. Obviously any such permutation must have an increment that is prime to n, and almost as obvious that any such increment will work, with any starting value; hence phi(n) * n total. - _Franklin T. Adams-Watters_, Jun 09 2009
%C A002618 Consider the numbers from 1 to n^2 written line by line as an n X n square: a(n) = number of numbers that are coprime to all their horizontal and vertical immediate neighbors. - _Reinhard Zumkeller_, Apr 12 2011
%C A002618 n -> a(n) is injective: a(m) = a(n) implies m = n. - _Franz Vrabec_, Dec 12 2012
%C A002618 a(p) = p*(p-1) a pronic number, see A036689 and A002378. - _Fred Daniel Kline_, Mar 30 2015
%C A002618 Conjecture: All the rational numbers sum_{i=j,...,k} 1/a(i) with 0 < min{2,k} <= j <= k have pairwise distinct fractional parts. - _Zhi-Wei Sun_, Sep 24 2015
